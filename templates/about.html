About

HANSEL is a companion to GRETIL, focused on the open exchange of new and newly modified Sanskrit e-text material, especially diplomatic transcriptions of printed editions produced using OCR.

FAQ

Expand All
  • Will HANSEL incorporate all of GRETIL?
    • No, HANSEL's focus is on new or newly modified material. HANSEL has particular requirements of structure and quality that make mass-import of GRETIL infeasible. That said, anyone wishing to improve upon a given GRETIL item can do that on HANSEL. In the meantime, GRETIL will have no problem remaining publicly available online in the form of its official archive on TextGrid (see post on Indology) and, when the main site eventually goes down, through mirror sites.
  • How do I use the site?
    • The getting-started videos should answer most questions. (Let me know if not!)
  • How do I contribute?
    • Just send us a message using the contact form. Whatever state your project is in, the library curator will help you from there.
  • How does HANSEL compare to other online Sanskrit e-text collections?
    • Like GRETIL, HANSEL is all-purpose and easy to contribute to. Like SARIT, HANSEL has an explicit data model, everything is versioned in real-time using git and GitHub, and thorough metadata is required. Like Muktabodha, HANSEL's base format is plain text, but this is then automatically converted to XML and HTML. Like DCS, HANSEL anticipates computational applications.
  • Is this project sustainable?
    • HANSEL is a labor of love by a committed, technically skilled, and financially independent creator. It is unaffiliated, open-source, well documented, and built with standard and inexpensive technologies.

Backronym and Logo

"Human-accessible" means that material will always exist in forms that the greatest number of students and scholars can use directly, such as plain-text and HTML.

"NLP-ready" means that the data will also be structured in anticipation of computational applications. (NLP stands for “natural language processing.”)

The haṃsa (swan, or bar-headed goose) symbolizes not only grace but also discernment (viveka). A world away, in the Grimms' tale, Hänsel and Gretel succeed against the odds by working together.
